When we start learning about a new technology, the best way to maintain both, consistency and interest is too surround ourselves with resources/instincts concerned with the technology. I’ve personally found this technique very useful for both knowledge acquisition and overall development as a developer. Here are some ways in which we can apply this technique to any new technology that we wish to start learning,

![AltText](images/image_1.jpg)

> Let’s assume that person A, a university student studying CS like us, wants to start learning a new programming language, Rust. Note, Rust is not a mainstream language like Python, Java or C++ and has lesser audience compared to the former languages. Just like every other new technology, Rust has a promising future and person A wishes to ride the rising wave. 

## 1. 🎯 A Primary Resource

In order to start learning, a primary resource must be selected from where 80% of the learning would occur. This could be a Udemy course, a book, official docs or a YT channel with tutorials. Person’s A learning path must coincide with the course’s flow, as it would help maintain consistency. Along with the primary resource, we need some ‘more’ that would person A maintain fascinating in learning Rust.

## 2. 👍 Like, Share and Subscribe

![AltText](images/image_3.jpg)

For each major programming language, there are lots of YT channels that are share tips, hands-on coding session and how-to(s) effectively to build audiences. Subscribing to a few channels would bring in tips or facts that are ‘out-of-box’ and can help maintain fascination towards the topic. For person A, learning Rust from the rust-lang book (primary resource) might just become boring until news/videos like ‘Building a super-fast web server in Rust’, ‘Linux chooses Rust as the 2nd language for its kernel after C’ come in which super-charge person A’s fascination. Subscribing to YT channels is always a good-to-go option for learning and motivation. Also, person A need not worry if a particular framework or concept seems complicated or difficult now, as with gradual learning everything would be fine. Also, when we are in-contact with the latest news/topics around the technology we’re trying to learn, a positive perception gets created suggesting that ‘there’s more to do ahead, and I can build cutting-edge things after learning this’.

## 3. 🔊 Forums

![AltText](images/image_2.jpg)

Following videos, forums are another good option to consider. By visiting forums, person A can get to you what professional developers are trying to do with that technology i.e. Rust and what difficulties they’re trying to solve. Honestly, for absolute beginners, forums might look like something completely out-of-the-world, as discussions are held technically and less oriented towards learning. The good side of forums is that, they can provide excellent explanations or resources for topics which are poorly covered in the primary resources or not understood by the learner. Even by having a look at the code posted by developers on these forums, the style of coding or design-patterns could be understood, or more generally, we get to know ‘how things really work outside of tutorials?’. Some popular forums like StackOverflow, Reddit, Discord servers or community-specific discussion groups (usually hosted by the maintainers of the technology)

## 4. 🥳 Peer groups - Friends!

![AltText](images/image_4.jpg)

Once we’ve surrounded ourselves with forums and YT channels that accelerate our learning, the next step could be to find a peer group who are trying to learn the same technology. Person A can probably send a message on their college’s discussion channels stating that “Hi everyone! I’ve started learning Rust recently and it looks interesting and fun to code. Is there anyone else who is interested in learning Rust or wishes to start?”. Learning is greatly enhanced with a peer goal who have similar goals and interests, and is a well-established fact even outside of programming.

## 5. 🧑‍🔬 Content Creators on LinkedIn and Twitter

![AltText](images/image_5.jpg)

Also, we do have content creators on LinkedIn or Twitter who share useful resources on the technology of interest. Following such creators would not only help in learning, but would also help in better, productive engagement with those social platforms. By doing so, we could transform LinkedIn/Twitter into a learning platform, where we could also approach experts or other enthusiasts.

## 🛑 Beware!

After following these points, a common problem that may be arise is “which resource should I follow to learn XYZ technology?” which is evident due to the large exposure we’ve created just now. To mitigate this problem, a student must have complete faith over their primary resource and if any ‘holes’ are identified within the primary resource (or some expert has pointed out those for us), it is our responsibility to fill out those holes neatly and in-place, such that they cause no confusion in related or nearer topics.
